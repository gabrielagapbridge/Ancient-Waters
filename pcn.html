01The Mystery

Scattered across California's Coast Range — from the Oregon border to Santa Barbara — are boulders carved with a strange, repetitive motif: circular and oval grooves pecked into rock, leaving a raised center, or nucleus. They look like nothing else in North American rock art.

Nobody knows who made them. Nobody knows exactly why. The carvings predate every tribal boundary we can identify from the contact era. They cross the territories of Coast Miwok, Ohlone, Pomo, Wappo, and dozens of other peoples who arrived in these landscapes thousands of years after the carvings were made.

In 1976, Teresa Miller and Reed Haslam gave them a name: Pecked Curvilinear Nucleated (PCN). Miller's 1977 San Francisco State thesis used a predictive model based on geology to discover approximately sixty previously unreported sites. Donna Lee Gillette's 2011 UC Berkeley dissertation remains the most comprehensive study, examining sites across 2,150 hectares of the Hopland Research and Extension Center in Mendocino County.

What we know is tantalizing. What we don't know is vast.

03What Makes Them Unique

PCN carvings are unlike any other rock art tradition in western North America. Here's what sets them apart:

The Motif

Circular or oval grooves pecked into rock, creating a raised center (nucleus). Sometimes accompanied by cupules (small ground cups). The basic elements are circles and ovals with nuclei that appear raised. They seldom occur in any discernible pattern.

The Technique

Pecked, not painted or incised. The carver struck the rock surface repeatedly with a hammerstone to create grooves. This is a subtractive process — material is removed to define the shape. Some researchers have proposed the technique itself may be a form of soft-rock quarrying.

The Rock

Almost exclusively found on chlorite schist and blueschist — metamorphic rocks created in ancient subduction zones. These rocks only surface along fault lines, which means PCN sites are geologically constrained in a way no other rock art tradition is.

The Water

PCN sites are almost always found near water sources — creeks, springs, seeps. The association is so strong that Miller used proximity to water as a predictor variable in her 1977 site-discovery model, which correctly identified dozens of new sites.

The Distribution

A narrow corridor along the California Coast Range from southern Oregon to Santa Barbara. PCN carvings do not appear in the Sierra Nevada, the Great Basin, the Central Valley, or anywhere outside this specific geological zone — reinforcing the rock-type connection.

The Age

Estimated 3,000–8,000 years before present. Ring Mountain (the type site) may be 5,000–8,000 years old. This predates every known tribal boundary in the region by millennia. The makers were likely Hokan or early Penutian speakers whose identity is lost to time.

04Deep Time

To understand how old PCN carvings are, consider what else was happening in the world — and what hadn't happened yet on this landscape:

~8,000 BP (6000 BCE)
Earliest possible PCN carvings. Sea levels are still rising from the last ice age. San Francisco Bay is just beginning to flood. The Sacramento–San Joaquin Delta doesn't exist yet. Agriculture hasn't been invented in the Americas.
~5,000 BP (3000 BCE)
Ring Mountain carvings estimated. Egypt is building the Great Pyramids. The wheel has just been invented. In California, PCN carvers are pecking circular grooves into blueschist boulders overlooking a bay that's only half its current size.
~3,000 BP (1000 BCE)
Latest estimated PCN activity. Homer is composing the Iliad. Iron Age begins in Europe. By this time, the PCN tradition appears to have ended — or evolved into something unrecognizable.
~2,400 BP (400 BCE)
Oldest mortar holes and habitation artifacts at Ring Mountain. These are from later peoples — Coast Miwok ancestors — who lived among the carvings but did not make them.
~1,500 BP (500 CE)
Coast Miwok, Ohlone, and Pomo cultures are well-established in their historic territories. The PCN carvings are already ancient — as old to these peoples as Roman ruins are to us.
1579 CE
Francis Drake lands at Point Reyes. The PCN carvings on Ring Mountain are at least 4,000 years old. Nobody records them.
1976 CE
Teresa Miller and Reed Haslam coin the term "PCN" and begin systematic study. Miller's 1977 thesis at San Francisco State University uses geology to predict and discover ~60 new sites.
2011 CE
Donna Lee Gillette completes her UC Berkeley dissertation — the most comprehensive PCN study to date, analyzing 30+ sites and 3,000+ artifacts at Hopland Research Center.

05The Geology Connection

Perhaps the most striking thing about PCN carvings is their geological specificity. While most rock art traditions will use whatever stone is available — sandstone, basalt, granite — PCN carvers sought out a very particular type of rock.

Chlorite Schist & Blueschist

PCN carvings appear almost exclusively on chlorite schist — a green-blue metamorphic rock — and blueschist (glaucophane schist). These are among the rarest exposed rocks on Earth's surface. They form only under extreme conditions: when oceanic crust is subducted beneath continental crust at high pressure but relatively low temperature (200–500°C at depths of 15–30 km). The rock is then thrust back to the surface along fault zones.

In California, these rocks are part of the Franciscan Complex — remnants of oceanic crust scraped off the subducting Pacific Plate over the last 200 million years. They surface as isolated outcrops and "knockers" along fault zones throughout the Coast Range. M.C. Blake Jr. (1985) mapped the distribution of blueschist in the San Francisco Bay region specifically in connection with rock art research.

The blueschist belt extends over 800 km along the eastern margin of the Coast Range — and PCN sites follow this belt almost exactly.

Why this rock? Theories range from practical (it's softer and easier to carve than granite) to spiritual (the blue-green color and fault-zone origins may have held sacred significance). Central Pomo mythology connects PCN/cupule boulders to fertility rituals — these "Baby Rocks" were visited by childless women who would fast for four days, then commune with spirits of unborn children believed to dwell within the stone.

Reed Haslam (1986) proposed that at least some PCN elements are the byproduct of soft-rock quarrying — that the material removed from the grooves was the desired product, perhaps ground into powder for ceremonial use. The powder from certain metamorphic rocks has documented use in indigenous fertility and weather rituals throughout California.

07Open Questions

PCN rock art remains one of the great unsolved puzzles of California archaeology. Here's what we still don't know:

Who made them?

The carvers predated every identifiable contact-era tribe. They may have been early Hokan speakers — the oldest language family in California — or an even earlier population. No skeletal remains or habitation sites have been definitively linked to PCN carvers.

Why this specific rock?

The chlorite schist / blueschist specificity is extraordinary. Was it practical (easier to carve)? Spiritual (the blue-green color, the origin from deep within the earth)? Medicinal (the powder produced by pecking)? All three?

Were the nucleated centers removed intentionally?

Some researchers note that the raised centers in PCN carvings could have been removed to create charmstones — polished stone objects found across California. If true, the "art" we see may actually be the negative impression of a manufacturing process.

Do PCN sites follow fault lines?

The geological constraint is clear, but nobody has done a systematic GIS comparison of PCN site locations against detailed fault maps. If the correlation is as tight as it appears, it would suggest the carvers had a sophisticated understanding of geology — or that fault zones held spiritual power.

Are there undiscovered sites?

Teresa Miller's 1977 predictive model found ~60 new sites by looking for the right rock type near water. Given how much of the Coast Range remains unsurveyed, there could be hundreds of undocumented PCN sites. Many are likely on private land, hidden in backyards, buried under development, or lost to quarrying.

What is the relationship to entoptic phenomena?

The circular, concentric patterns of PCN carvings resemble "entoptic" or "phosphene" imagery — visual patterns generated by the nervous system during altered states of consciousness (fasting, trance, psychoactive plants). If PCN carvers were recording vision-quest experiences, the tradition would connect to shamanic practices documented across California — but the link remains speculative.

Is this a single tradition or multiple convergent practices?

Over a span of 5,000 years and 200+ miles, did one continuous cultural tradition produce all PCN carvings? Or did multiple groups independently discover that pecking circles into blueschist produced useful or meaningful results?
