‚ú¶ Theories of Rock Art Interpretation

Competing frameworks for understanding why prehistoric peoples created rock art in the Great Basin

üèπ

Hunting Magic

Heizer & Baumhoff 1962 ¬∑ Steward 1929, 1938

The earliest systematic theory of Great Basin rock art. Heizer and Baumhoff argued that petroglyphs were placed along game migration routes and near hunting blinds to supernaturally aid in the procurement of game animals ‚Äî particularly bighorn sheep and deer. Rock art imagery was suggested to depict hunting scenes, game animals, and fertility themes.

Key evidence: Spatial association of rock art with game trails, hunting blinds, and ambush points. Prevalence of animal imagery at some sites.

Critique: Quinlan & Woody (2003) argue this model is too restrictive ‚Äî it limits rock art's audience to hunters and struggles to account for the prevalence of abstract imagery or the domestic settlement context of many sites.

üåÄ

Shamanism & Neuropsychological Model

Whitley 1994, 1998 ¬∑ Lewis-Williams 1981, 1988

Drawing on neuropsychological research into altered states of consciousness and the cross-cultural anthropology of shamanism, this approach argues that Great Basin rock art predominantly represents shamanic metaphors and vision-quest experiences. Sites functioned as vision-quest locales where supernatural powers and visions could be obtained, used exclusively by shamans.

Key evidence: Entoptic (geometric) imagery consistent with trance states. Ethnographic accounts of shamans and rock art. Sites in remote, secluded locations consistent with vision-quest retreats.

Critique: Vigorous debate exists over the degree of ethnographic support for this model. Quinlan & Woody argue the domestic context of much rock art opens the possibility that it was encountered by a broad cross-section of society, not just ritual specialists.

üî•

Social Reproduction & Ethnic Identity

Quinlan & Woody 2003 ¬∑ Bradley 2000

Quinlan and Woody propose that rock art functioned as a powerful symbolic resource embedded in ritual, used to negotiate social relationships, legitimize access to land and resources, and mediate social tensions. The landscape context ‚Äî particularly the close association of rock art with domestic settlements ‚Äî suggests it was incorporated into community-wide rituals, not restricted to specialized practitioners.

Key evidence: In Nevada, of 1,037 recorded rock art sites, lithics were reported at 188 sites and ground stone at 114. Approximately 40% of studied sites in southern Nevada showed domestic debris. The predominantly abstract character of Great Basin rock art (Basin and Range Tradition) suggests concern with internal social communication.

Numic dispersal connection: Rock art patterns map onto the spread of Numic-speaking peoples across the Great Basin. The earliest art is abstract (Basin and Range Tradition, dated to before 6800 B.P.), while incoming Numic populations may have modified existing rock art through scratching ‚Äî potentially to "re-socialize" the landscape. The near-total absence of rock art production among historic Numic peoples suggests it belonged to preceding populations with different social organizations.

Rock Art Styles & the Numic Dispersal

Synthesis from Quinlan & Woody 2003 ¬∑ Bettinger & Baumhoff 1982 ¬∑ Schaafsma 1971, 1980
Before 6800 B.P.
Basin and Range Tradition
Abstract geometric motifs ‚Äî circles, curvilinear meanders, vulviforms, squares. The earliest and most widespread style, deeply revarnished at sites like Grimes Point and Long Lake, Oregon. Emphasis on abstract expression may indicate concern with internal social communication rather than naturalistic representation.
ca. 1000‚Äì1300 A.D.
Fremont & Anasazi Figurative Traditions
Broad-shouldered anthropomorphs with horns or elaborate headgear, alongside bighorn sheep. Associated with semi-agricultural economies in Utah and the Southwest. Stylistic connections between Fremont, Western Kayenta, and Coso Range art suggest cultural linkages along the Colorado River drainage.
ca. 1000‚Äì1500 A.D.
Numic Expansion & Scratched Art
As Numic-speaking peoples spread from the Death Valley area into the eastern and southwestern Great Basin, incoming populations may have produced scratched rock art to "re-socialize" the landscape and its existing monuments. Once the landscape was re-socialized, rock art production became sporadic and its memory was lost.
Contact Period
Historic Imagery
Depictions of horses, cowboys, and wagons ‚Äî the only rock art clearly attributable to historic Numic populations. Sporadic and idiosyncratic in distribution, probably not part of a larger cultural institution.
Primary source: Quinlan, Angus R., and Alanah Woody. 2003. "Marks of Distinction: Rock Art and Ethnic Identification in the Great Basin." American Antiquity 68(2): 372‚Äì390.

‚ú¶ Classification & Typology

How archaeologists categorize Great Basin rock art ‚Äî by what it depicts, which cultural tradition produced it, and how it was made

‚óâ

By Motif ‚Äî What the Image Depicts

‚Ä∫
The most fundamental classification divides rock art by subject matter. Abstract/geometric imagery dominates the Great Basin ‚Äî roughly 80% of all recorded motifs ‚Äî while representational figures (humans, animals, scenes) make up the remainder. The deliberate prevalence of ambiguous abstract designs likely made their meanings a form of controlled, special knowledge.

Curvilinear Abstract

~31% OF ALL MOTIFS
10,000 B.P. ‚Üí Contact
The single most common motif family. Includes circles, concentric circles, connected circles, spirals, dots, serpentine lines, curvilinear meanders, and wavy lines. These appear to be the earliest and most persistent design vocabulary in the Great Basin. Some researchers (Lewis-Williams, Dowson) connect these to entoptic phenomena ‚Äî geometric form constants hardwired into the human visual system that appear during altered states of consciousness.
Sites: Found at virtually every Great Basin rock art site

Rectilinear Abstract

~25% OF ALL MOTIFS
10,000 B.P. ‚Üí Contact
Includes grids, rectangles, rakes, cross-hatching, zigzags, ladders, and straight parallel lines. Always co-occurs with curvilinear motifs at the same sites but is generally less abundant. Together, curvilinear and rectilinear abstracts form the backbone of the Basin and Range Tradition.
Sites: Co-occurs with curvilinear at all major sites

Anthropomorphs (Human-like Figures)

~5% OF MOTIFS
Variable ‚Äî regionally and temporally restricted styles are key cultural markers
Though a small percentage of total imagery, anthropomorphs are critically important because their distinctive stylistic treatments are the best way to identify cultural affiliation. Simple stick figures are universal, but several regionally restricted, formally distinct styles carry deep cultural-historical significance:
Stick-Figure Anthropomorphs

The simplest and most widespread form. Found across the entire Great Basin at all time periods. Sometimes shown with exaggerated hands, fingers, feet, or toes. Occasionally phallic or bearing weapons.

Fremont Anthropomorphs

Triangular, trapezoidal, or rectangular torsos, often lacking legs, sometimes with headgear, "horns," ear decoration, or internal body patterns. Found in eastern/southern Nevada and Utah. Associated with Fremont semi-horticultural cultures (ca. 1500‚Äì800 B.P.). The Sevier substyle closely resembles Fremont clay figurines.

Western Puebloan (Kayenta)

Triangular, rectangular, or hourglass-shaped bodies, sometimes with horns. Found in Valley of Fire, lower Moapa Valley, western Utah. Associated with Ancestral Puebloan groups. Stylistically overlaps with but is visually distinct from Fremont forms.

Pahranagat Anthropomorphs

Unique to Pahranagat Valley, SE Nevada. Two types: (1) Rectangular "fringed" body with internal grids/dots, often headless with stick legs; (2) Solid-pecked ovoid body with large eyes in negative space and a head protrusion. Contemporary Great Basin Native communities identify these as the spirit figure "Water Baby" and their associated shamans' helpers.

Coso Patterned-Body

Elaborate figures with internally decorated bodies at the Coso Range, eastern California. Show stylistic connections to both Fremont and Anasazi rock art traditions, suggesting cultural linkages along the Colorado River drainage rather than with interior Great Basin hunter-gatherer groups.

Zoomorphs (Animal Figures)

VARIABLE BY REGION
Bighorn sheep are overwhelmingly dominant, especially in eastern and southern Nevada. Their symbolic importance likely exceeded their dietary role ‚Äî small mammals, deer, antelope, and especially plants were more important food sources. Other animals include deer, elk, coyotes, mountain lions, lizards, and ambiguous serpentine forms (possible snakes). Zoomorphs are more prominent in southern/eastern Nevada and less common in the north and west.

Representational Scenes

RARE
Hunting scenes (a human with bow beside a bighorn sheep) are the most recognizable narrative compositions. Interestingly, atlatl hunting scenes are virtually unknown despite the atlatl's long use ‚Äî when shown, atlatls are usually held but not used. This is a key dating marker: bow-and-arrow scenes postdate ~1,500 years ago. Other scenes include depictions of material culture (dart points ‚Äî extremely rare) and possible ceremonial compositions.

Cupules (Pit-and-Groove)

DISTINCT FORM
Hemispherical cup-shaped depressions, usually a few centimeters wide and deep, pecked or ground into boulders. Once believed to be the earliest Great Basin rock art style, though this dating is now debated. Distinctively different from all other motif types and likely produced for an entirely different purpose. Among the oldest known petroglyph forms worldwide (Bhimbetka cupules in India date to 200,000‚Äì700,000 B.P.).

By Style & Tradition ‚Äî Cultural-Chronological Classification

‚Ä∫
Style classification intersects motif with time and cultural identity. Each tradition reflects not just aesthetic choices but distinct peoples, economies, and social organizations. Identifying a rock art style at a site can reveal who made it, roughly when, and what kind of society they lived in.

Great Basin Carved Abstract (GBCA)

OLDEST KNOWN
ca. 13,000‚Äì9,000 B.P. (Terminal Pleistocene / Early Holocene)
The oldest identified style. Characterized by deeply carved, dense geometric patterns with wide lines and very little negative space. Best known from Winnemucca Lake, Nevada ‚Äî potentially the oldest scientifically dated rock art in North America (10,500‚Äì14,800 years old). Concentrated in the northern Great Basin and not widely distributed.
Key site: Winnemucca Lake, NV

Great Basin Pecked (GBP)

LONGEST-LIVED
ca. 11,000‚Äì13,000 B.P. ‚Üí 20th century
Originated in the southern Great Basin and continued for over 10,000 years. Encompasses simple geometric designs plus stylized representational motifs ‚Äî commonly bighorn sheep and anthropomorphs. Recent rock varnish dating confirms this style was present in the Snake River Valley by at least 10,000 B.P.
Key sites: Celebration Park (ID), Snake River Valley

Basin and Range Tradition

MOST WIDESPREAD
ca. 8,000‚Äì150 B.P. (Archaic through Late Prehistoric)
The most common and widespread style. The full range of curvilinear and rectilinear abstract motifs, plus stick-figure anthropomorphs and zoomorphs. Strongly associated with Archaic hunter-gatherer cultures. Its enormous time depth and geographic spread make finer chronological subdivision extremely difficult. Abstract designs account for ~80% of imagery in this tradition.
Key sites: Grimes Point, Massacre Lake, most Nevada sites

Fremont Rock Art

EASTERN NV / UTAH
ca. 1,500‚Äì800 B.P. (A.D. 500‚Äì1200)
Associated with the Fremont archaeological culture ‚Äî semi-horticultural peoples with variable reliance on wild resources. Distinguished by broad-shouldered anthropomorphs with elaborate headgear, body decoration, and occasional horns. The Sevier Fremont substyle uses highly schematic triangular/trapezoidal forms. Fremont people were distinct from and eventually replaced by Numic-speaking hunter-gatherers.
Key sites: Katchina Cave, eastern NV; Fremont sites across Utah

Ancestral Puebloan (Western Kayenta)

SOUTHERN NV / COLORADO DRAINAGE
ca. 1,500‚Äì700 B.P. (A.D. 500‚Äì1300)
Associated with Puebloan horticultural groups. Triangular, rectangular, or hourglass-shaped anthropomorphs sometimes with horns or headgear. Overlaps chronologically with Fremont but is stylistically distinct. Found in the Valley of Fire, lower Moapa Valley, and into the Arizona Strip. Both Fremont and Anasazi groups developed in response to horticultural economies that collapsed during 12th‚Äì13th century drought.
Key sites: Lost City (NV), Valley of Fire

Grapevine Canyon Style

SOUTHERN NV / MOJAVE
Age uncertain
A distinctive abstract style found along the Colorado River drainage south of Las Vegas Valley, extending into the eastern Mojave Desert and Arizona Strip. Composed of symmetrical, rectilinear elements forming complex geometric motifs that use negative space as essential design components. Unique visual character sets it apart from Basin and Range Tradition abstracts.
Key site: Grapevine Canyon, NV

Coso Tradition

COSO RANGE, CA
Produced over a very long period; majority argued to be more recent
Characterized by large numbers of bighorn sheep and elaborate patterned-body anthropomorphs. The Coso Range is the most striking exception to the general Great Basin pattern of abstract-dominated art. Stylistic similarities to Fremont and Anasazi art suggest cultural connections along the Colorado drainage. Whether this reflects revitalization movements, semi-sedentary community development, or other processes is debated.
Key site: Little Petroglyph Canyon, Coso Range, CA

Scratched Rock Art

NUMIC EXPANSION?
ca. 1,000‚Äì500 B.P. (possibly earlier)
Fine incised lines rather than pecking. Bettinger & Baumhoff (1982) associated scratched art with incoming Numic populations, arguing it was intended to "obliterate" pre-existing art and re-socialize the landscape. However, scratched art isn't always superimposed over older art ‚Äî sometimes it enhances or embellishes existing designs. In California, it predominantly takes the form of defacement. Age is difficult to determine since rock varnish may not form the same way on scratched surfaces.

Historic Period

POST-CONTACT
ca. A.D. 1700 ‚Üí 1900s
Depictions of horses, cowboys, wagons, and other post-contact subjects. The only rock art clearly attributable to historic Great Basin Native populations. Sporadic and idiosyncratic in distribution ‚Äî probably not part of a larger cultural institution. Its production may have been revived by the experience of colonialism (Quinlan & Woody 2003).
Key site: Stuart Ranch, NV

By Production Technique ‚Äî How It Was Made

‚Ä∫
The method of production provides clues about tools available, time invested, cultural intent, and relative dating. Petroglyphs (carved) vastly outnumber pictographs (painted) in the Great Basin, largely because they survive better in open desert environments.

Pecked

MOST COMMON
A hammerstone strikes the rock surface directly or through a chisel stone, creating small pits that are interconnected to form designs. The most labor-intensive technique ‚Äî elaborate petroglyphs required many perfectly placed hits and untold hours. Removes dark desert varnish to expose lighter rock beneath. Can be fully pecked (solid) or outline-pecked.

Engraved / Incised

COMMON
A sharp lithic tool cuts narrow lines into the rock surface. Produces finer, more controlled lines than pecking. Well-suited for detailed designs and internal body decoration on anthropomorphs.

Abraded / Ground

COMMON
A stone is rubbed against the rock surface to create smooth, ground-down areas. Sometimes used to prepare surfaces before other techniques are applied. Can create broader, smoother marks than pecking or incising.

Scratched

FINE / SHALLOW
Fine lines scratched with a sharp tool. Produces the shallowest, least visible marks. Potentially associated with Numic populations. Can be difficult to distinguish from natural striations, and its true age is challenging to determine since rock varnish may regrow differently on scratched versus pecked surfaces.

Painted (Pictographs)

LESS COMMON IN GREAT BASIN
Pigments ‚Äî hematite/ochre for red, kaolin/gypsum for white, charcoal for black ‚Äî mixed with binders (animal fats, plant oils) and applied with brushes (animal hair, yucca fiber) or fingers. Much rarer in the Great Basin than petroglyphs because they survive poorly in open settings. Best preserved in caves and rock shelters. Pictographs can be radiocarbon dated (unlike petroglyphs), though sampling is destructive.

Combined Techniques

OCCASIONAL
Some panels show multiple techniques layered over time: pecked outlines filled with paint, painted figures later outlined by incision, or scratched additions to older pecked art. These "painted petroglyphs" and palimpsests of techniques provide evidence of sites being revisited and reworked across generations ‚Äî sometimes over thousands of years.
